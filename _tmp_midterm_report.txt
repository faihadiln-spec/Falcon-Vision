1. Introduction 
Falcon Vision is an AI-powered vision system integrated with a large language model (LLM) to 
enhance industrial safety monitoring. The system combines computer vision with natural 
language processing to detect safety violations and ensure compliance with company-specific 
safety regulations. Our main objective is to develop an intelligent monitoring system that 
supports real-time safety compliance and violation detection.  
The report presents the progress achieved so far in the development of the system, along with the 
implementation details. It also highlights any changes from the proposal phase and the 
challenges encountered during the development process.

2. Summary of the proposal 
The proposed Falcon Vision is an AI-based system designed to improve industrial safety 
monitoring. The main idea is to build a system that can interpret safety regulations provided by 
the companies and use them to guide workplace monitoring.  
Safety documents are processed using large language models to extract structured safety rules 
and requirements. These extracted rules will define what should be monitored in the 
environment, such as compliance with PPE, detection of unsafe behavior such as falls, 
identification of workers in restricted areas, and detection of hazardous conditions such as fires. 
After extracting rules, computer vision models are used to analyze the video streams to detect 
safety compliance and violations in real-time. Then, the output of these models is compared 
against the extracted rules to determine whether a safety violation has occurred.  
The system is designed to be adaptable to different industrial environments by allowing each 
company to provide its own safety regulations.

This section outlines the implementation process of our project, which covers five main tasks. 
Each task has its own dataset and corresponding model designed to address specific objectives 
4.1 Large Language Models (LLMs) 
4.1.1 Collected Dataset 
The dataset used in this part of the project. 17 safety handbooks were obtained from the web. 
The safety handbooks obtained are from different sectors, such as: 
• Constructions 
• Energy 
• oil and gas 
After reading these handbooks and filtering, we've had only 8 handbooks as a dataset from 
different industrial sectors, containing: three from the energy sector, three from the 
construction sector, and two from the oil and gas sector. 
Since we've developed a pre-trained model, we didn't divide them as train- test sets; we've 
used them all to evaluate the model with our specialized instructions. 
 
4.1.2 Model and techniques 
In this section, we employed a hybrid approach combining a text parser with a Large 
Language model for document rules extraction. We've employed two pipelines: 
Docling Parser → GPT-oss-20 and Llamaparse → GPT-oss-20b to compare which pipeline 
yields the most accurate results. 
GPT‑oss‑20b is a large language model from OpenAI, open-weight models for reasoning. It's 
used for a specialized use case with 21B parameters [1]. 
1. Docling+GPT-oss-20b: 
The first text parser is an efficient open-source tool from IBM research that excels at cleanly 
extracting text and tables into Markdown. It has privacy because it runs locally without 
sending data to the cloud [2]. 
1. Docling reads the PDF and extracts text, tables, and preserves the layout structure. 
2. After parsing, Docling converts the extracted PDF content into a readable markdown 
format. 
3. Divided the markdown file into chunks. 
4. The GPT model uses these chunks to extract the rules. 
 
2. Llamaparse+GPT-oss-20b:

After the document is converted to Markdown and divided into chunks, a targeted prompt is 
used to guide the GPT-oss-20b model. This ensures the model acts as a Safety Auditor rather

The prompt consists of three key components: 
• The Persona & Context: Instructs the model to analyze the text specifically for safety 
regulations. 
• The Targeted Questions: 
1. PPE: Identifying minimum required equipment for workers. 
2. Fall Protection: Requirements for harnesses, height safety, and ladders. 
3. Heat Limits: Specific temperature thresholds (e.g., Celsius/WBGT) that trigger 
work stoppages or rest breaks. 
• The Output Constraint: Forces the model to return a structured JSON object. This allows 
the extracted rules to be programmatically saved and compared across both the Docling 
and LlamaParse pipelines.

that the reported accuracy accurately reflects not only the model's ability to identify text but 
also its ability to correctly determine and classify safety regulations, without redundant 
overlaps or hallucinations. 
The following metrics were used to quantify the performance of the pipeline: 
• True Positives (TP): Rules correctly extracted from the PDF.

4.2 PPE Detection 
4.2.1 Collected Dataset 
The required PPE was first extracted from the safety documents to identify the protection 
equipment needed for each company. Since different companies request varying PPE, we 
selected the most frequently mentioned and commonly required PPE from these documents to 
ensure general applicability across multiple environments. Moreover, using these selected PPE, 
we created a dataset with 20 classes, including 10 PPE and 10 violations. The classes are: 
• Coverall / No Coverall  
• Ear Protectors / No Ear Protectors 
• Face Shield / No Face Shield 
• Gloves / No Gloves 
• Helmet / No Helmet 
• Mask / No Mask 
• Safety Glasses / No Safety Glasses 
• Safety Harness / No Safety Harness 
• Safety Shoes / No Safety Shoes 
• Safety Vest / No Safety Vest 
As no existing dataset included all these classes, we created a custom dataset [4] by manually 
collecting and combining data from multiple sources, mainly Roboflow. Then, we labeled the 
dataset, applied preprocessing, and data augmentation in Roboflow. To have a final dataset with 
13284 images divided into 80% training (10712 images), 11% validation (1418 images), and 9% 
testing (1154 images).  
For preprocessing, all images were auto-oriented to correct the image alignment and resized to 
768 x 768 to standardize the input dimensions. In addition, below are the data augmentations that 
were applied to the dataset: 
• Zoom: Images randomly zoomed up to 25%. 
• Rotation: Images were rotated between -15% and 15%. 
• Saturation: Changing the color intensity between -20% and 20%. 
• Brightness: Changing the lighting of the image between -15% and 15%. 
• Exposure: Simulate the camera lighting variation between -10% and 10%. 
• Mosaic: Combine multiple images into one image.

4.2.2 Model and techniques 
For the PPE detection, we selected YOLO26 as the primary object detection. It’s one of the latest 
versions of the YOLO family, designed to provide high accuracy while maintaining efficient 
real-time performance. It also improves the small object detection, removes the need for Non-
Maximum Suppression (NMS) step, and optimized for real-world applications, achieving faster 
inference even on CPU-based systems [5]. In addition, it offers a great balance between accuracy 
and speed, making it suitable for complex scenes with multiple objects, while still supporting 
real-time monitoring. 
YOLO26 is available in different sizes (n, s, m, l, x), where each provides a different speed and 
accuracy tradeoff. We selected the medium (m) version that provides better accuracy than small 
variants while remaining efficient for real-time use. 
The model was fine-tuned on our custom PPE dataset containing 20 classes. The training was 
conducted for 100 epochs with an image size of 768x768 and a batch size of 16. We used the 
AdamW optimizer to improve convergence, and early stopping with a patience of 10 epochs to 
prevent overfitting.  
 
4.2.3  Evaluation & Result 
The performance of our model was evaluated using the standard object detection metric on the 
validation and testing sets. The model achieved the following results: 
• Precision: 89% 
• Recall: 85% 
• mAP50: 91% 
• mAP50-95: 63% 
The results indicate that the model performs effectively for PPE detection, achieving high 
precision and strong overall detection performance. The metric demonstrates that the model is 
suitable for real-time safety monitoring applications. Figures 1 and 2 show the result of the 
model detection on sample videos.

4.3.2 Model and techniques 
We use a hybrid approach combining deep learning YOLOv8 Pose and machine learning 
Random Forest to detect human falls. 
1. Pose Estimation using YOLOv8 
A YOLOv8s-pose model from the Ultralytics library was used to extract human body keypoints 
from images. The model was initialized with pretrained weights and then fine-tuned on the 
project dataset. The training was performed using the following configuration: 
• Epochs: 50  
• Image size: 640  
• Batch size: 16  
The model performs human detection within each image, followed by the extraction of 17 body 
keypoints corresponding to major joints of the human body. These keypoints are then used to 
represent and analyze the overall human posture, enabling the distinction between normal 
activities and fall events. After training, the best performing weights were used to generate 
predictions on the test dataset.

2. Feature Extraction 
Instead of relying on deep learning outputs, we extracted geometric features from the detected 
keypoints to improve classification performance. The following features were computed: 
• Body Angle 
The body angle was calculated using the midpoint between the shoulders and the midpoint 
between the hips, where upright positions show a more vertical alignment and fallen positions 
appear more horizontal. 
• Aspect Ratio (Width / Height) 
The ratio between body width and height was computed, as fallen individuals tend to appear 
wider than they are tall.  
• Center Height 
The vertical position of the body center was normalized relative to image height. People who 
have fallen are usually very close to the ground. 
• Shoulder Slope  
The vertical difference between the left and right shoulders was measured. To indicate body tilt 
• Hip Slope 
The vertical difference between the left and right hips was calculated. To reflect body stability or 
imbalance. 
 
3. Classification using Random Forest 
The extracted features were used to train a Random Forest classifier to classify each sample into  
Fall or Not Fallen. The Random Forest model was configured with 300 trees to ensure robust 
learning and improved prediction performance. The maximum depth of each tree was set to 6 to 
prevent overfitting and maintain generalization. Additionally, a random state of 42 was used to 
ensure reproducibility of the results. Finally, the classifier was trained on the training set and 
evaluated on the test set.

4.4 Fire Detection  
4.4.1 Collected Dataset 
The dataset used in this part of the project consists of two datasets required for the multimodal 
system (Machine Learning classifier and YOLO).  
For the ML classifier, the "Indoor Fire Dataset with Distributed Multi -Sensor Nodes" was 
utilized from Mendeley Data [8]. This dataset comprises four fire experiments (each repeated 
three times) and three nuisance experiments (Ethanol repeated three times, Deodorant repeated 
two times, and Hairspray repeated one time). It consists of a total of 305,304 rows and 19 
columns. Each row represents the date, sensor ID, and time-series sensor measurements (CO₂, 
CO, H₂, humidity, particulate matter of different sizes, air temperature, and ultraviolet radiation 
(UV)), in addition to labels such as scenario_label, anomaly_label, and ternary_label. Since 
the dataset is large, only eight features were selected, including CO₂, CO, H₂, humidity, two 
particulate matter features (PM), temperature, volatile organic compounds (VOC), and 
ternary_label as the target variable. The target variable consists of three classes: background, 
which indicates a normal state; nuisance, which represents false alarms; and fire. The dataset 
was split into 64% for training, 16% for validation, and 20% for testing. Each subset was scaled 
using StandardScaler, and the target variable was encoded using LabelEncoder. Additionally, 
the dataset was cleaned and contains no missing values. 
For the YOLO model, the "Indoor Fire Smoke Dataset" was utilized from Zenodo [ 9]. This 
dataset consists of 5,000 RGB images of indoor environments with annotated objects for fire 
and smoke detection. Each image includes bounding box annotations to detect visible fire and 
smoke across various indoor environments, such as houses, offices, and buildings, making it 
suitable for YOLO -based object detection models.  The dataset is pre -divided into 70% for 
training (3,500 images), 15% for validation (750 images), a nd 15% for testing (750 images). 
The dataset contains no duplicates and no corrupted images. 
 
4.4.2 Model and techniques 
This part of the project aims to achieve early fire detection in an industrial environment by 
developing a multimodal system that combines a machine learning classifier with YOLO. 
 
1. Machine Learning: 
To handle sensor data, Logistic Regression was selected. This model has an extended version 
that can handle more than two classes, known as multinomial logistic regression, which makes 
it suitable for this case. The model is trained on sensor measurements consisting of eight 
features, and based on these inputs, it classifies the output into three classes: normal state, 
nuisance state, and fire state. 
After training, validation, and testing, the joblib library was utilized to save the trained model, 
scaler, and label encoder as a pickle file. This step is important for enabling the reuse of the 
model during the multimodal fusion phase. 
 
2. You Only Look Once (YOLO):

For detecting fire and smoke from indoor environment images, YOLOv8 was selected. This

3. Multimodal Fusion: 
This step represents the final stage of the multimodal system for early fire detection. The 
selected fusion approach is decision-level fusion (late fusion), where predictions from different 
models are combined after being generated [11]. 
Since this phase is still in progress, it will utilize the saved Logistic Regression classifier and 
YOLOv8n model to generate predictions from both datasets. After each model produces its 
prediction, a final decision is generated and interpreted as an alert. These alerts are categorized 
into four levels: No Alert, Low Alert, Medium Alert, and High Alert. 
4.4.3 Evaluation & Result 
Since different models are used in this part of the project, different evaluation metrics are 
applied to assess model performance. Both models are evaluated using a test set. 
 
1. Machine Learning Classifier Results: 
For the Logistic Regression classifier, the model achieves an overall accuracy of 96%. It 
classifies the background state with a precision of 95%, a recall of 100%, and an F1 -score of 
98%. For the fire state, the model achieves a precision of 100%, a recall of 76%, and an F1 -
score of 86%. For the nuisance state, the model achieves a precision of 93%, a recall of 74%, 
and an F1-score of 82%. 
In addition, a confusion matrix was generated, as shown in Figure [8]. The model misclassifies 
9 fire instances and 90 nuisance instances as background. During actual fire states, it misses 
1,756 fire cases and incorrectly predicts 64 of them as nuisance. Similarly, for nuisance states, 
no instances are misclassified as fire; however, 723 cases are misclassified as background. 
This confusion matrix indicates that the model performs very well in classifying the 
background class. However, the main limitation lies in detecting fire instances, where the 
number of false negatives (FN) is relatively high. Therefore, improving recall for fire detection 
is critical. Additionally, the model needs further improvement in classifying nuisance states.

4.5.1 Dataset and Model 
For the face recognition module, the LFW dataset was used as a benchmark dataset. The system 
was implemented using the InsightFace framework with the pretrained buffalo_l model, which 
combines face detection and recognition. 
The model extracts ArcFace embeddings, where each face is represented as a numerical feature 
vector that captures identity-related information. These embeddings are later used for comparing 
and recognizing individuals. 
To simulate a real-world access control scenario, the dataset was divided into training, 
validation, and testing sets using a ratio of 70%, 15%, and 15%, respectively. The training set 
was used to build the enrolled gallery, the validation set was used for threshold selection, and the 
test set was used for final evaluation. 
 
4.5.2 Recognition and Threshold Selection 
After extracting embeddings, face recognition was performed using cosine similarity between the 
probe image and the enrolled gallery embeddings. For each probe image, the system selects the 
identity with the highest similarity score from the enrolled gallery. 
Since the system is designed for access control, a decision threshold is required to determine 
whether a person should be accepted or rejected. Different threshold values were evaluated using 
the validation set to find the best balance between recognition performance and security. 
The selected threshold was 0.5095, as it achieved strong performance while maintaining a very 
low false acceptance rate.

4.5.3 Test and Open-Set Evaluation 
After choosing the threshold, the model was evaluated on the test set. The implemented system 
showed strong recognition performance. The Top-1 Identification Accuracy on the test set 
reached 97.08%, indicating that most probe images were matched with the correct enrolled 
identity. In addition, the Access-Decision Accuracy at the selected threshold reached 94.15%. 
The test results also showed a precision of nearly 100%, a recall of 93.98%, and a false 
acceptance rate of 0%. The performance metrics on the test set are shown in Figure 9. 
Achieving a false acceptance rate (FAR) of 0% is important in Falcon Vision since the system is 
used for access control in hazardous areas. It means that no unauthorized person was incorrectly 
accepted, which is critical for safety. While prioritizing security, the system still maintains strong 
recognition performance, showing a good balance between security and usability. 
To make the evaluation more realistic, an open-set scenario was also considered, since real 
access-control systems must reject unknown individuals who are not enrolled in the gallery. For 
this reason, impostor probes were generated from identities that were not part of the test gallery. 
These images were presented to the system to measure how often an unknown person would be 
incorrectly accepted. The system achieved an open-set false acceptance rate of 0%, while the true 
acceptance rate for authorized users remained 93.98%. These results indicate that the system is 
effective not only in recognizing enrolled identities, but also in rejecting unauthorized 
individuals.

6. Testing plan 
To ensure the reliability of the Falcon Vision system, we have developed a testing plan that 
focuses on evaluating each model component individually before they are integrated. 
6.1 Unit Testing of Models Component. 
Each core component is tested using its datasets and specific evaluation methods. 
• LLMs Testing: Using a set of 8 filtered safety handbooks, we test our pretrained models. 
To compare the capability of two different pipelines (Docling + GPT-oss-20b, 
LlamaParse + GPT-oss-20b) in extracting rules about PPE, fall protection, and heat 
limits, we test them. 
• PPE Detection Testing: We test the YOLO model on the test set of 1,154 pictures. The 
measure of performance is examined by determining whether the model successfully 
recognizes correctly all 20 classes, including gear, such as helmets and gloves, and the 
applicable safety violations. 
• Fall Detection Testing: 190 images are tested on the hybrid YOLOv8-Pose and Random 
Forest system. In particular, we observe the results to see that the model correctly 
differentiates between fall and non-fallen positions on the basis of 17 body keypoints. 
• Fire and Smoke Testing: 750 images are used to test the vision model and a 20 percent 
test split is used to test sensor-based ML classifier. We check the system ability to discern 
real fire conditions from those occurring as nuisance triggers. 
• Testing Face Recognition: The test set in our case is 15 percent of the LFW dataset, 
which is used to conduct testing. This is by testing the system capability to effectively 
match identities and deny unauthorized persons in an open-set setting. 
6.2 Performance Metrics 
We use the following metrics as the primary criteria to determine if our components are ready for 
deployment: 
• Precision & Recall: These are applicable to all the models because they are needed to 
make sure that the system is right and that it is not missing important safety rules and 
hazards. 
• F1-Score: This gives a balanced score of the fall detection and LLM components so that 
the retrieval and classification are similar. 
• mAP50 & mAP50-95: These are the standard values of measuring the overall accuracy of 
our object detection models of both PPE and fire. 
• Confusion Matrix: This is utilized to display the patterns of misclassification, including 
the detection of false alarms in the background or detection failures. 
• Top-1 & Access-Decision Accuracy: This is used to determine the success rate of the 
face recognition system in recognizing and making the right entry decision.

AI-based safety systems, covering multiple tasks:
